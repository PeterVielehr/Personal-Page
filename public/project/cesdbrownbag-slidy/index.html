Does Aggressive Policing Spillover onto Black Adults' Mental Health? <br> Depressive Symptoms, Proactive Policing, and Police Treatment in Nashville, TN

 

Background

Policing

  • Police work can be broken into two types
    • Reactive
    • Proactive
  • Reactive: Responding to calls for service and emergencies
  • Proactive: Seek out and deter crime
    • Strategies include:
      • Identifying and targeting hot spots
      • Increased high-visibility patrols
      • Pretextual traffic stops
      • Increased searches
    • Alternately called: “Hot Spot Policing,” “Problem Oriented Policing,” “Broken Windows Policing,” “Quality of Life Policing,” “Order Maintenance,” and “Zero-Tolerance”
      • (Braga and Weisburd 2010; Howell 2009; Kelling and Coles 1997; Rosenfeld, Fornango, and Rengifo 2007; Wilson and Kelling 1982)

Stop, Question, and Frisk

  • New York City practice of officers making pedestrian stops and frisking “suspects” for weapons.
    • Called “Terry Stops” from the Supreme Court Case, Terry v. Ohio 392 U.S. 1 (1968)

  • Figure 1: (New York Civil Liberties Union 2017)

  • Only 9% people stopped were white.

Effects of Aggressive, Proactive Policing

  • Disproportionate targeting of racial minority and low-income communities, especially black men.
    • More black and brown people in the criminal legal system.
    • Biases data used to justify tactics (Brayne 2017; Ferguson 2017)
  • Adversarial relationship between police and community.
    • Reduces trust in police
  • Reinforces an implicit assumption of guilt
  • Undermines belongingness for communities of color (Epp et al. 2014)
  • Creates a culture of state surveillance
  • (To be fair to criminologists) Meta-analyses of experimental studies testing location-based problem-oriented policing’s effect on crime show a small—yet statistically significant—crime reduction effect (Braga et al. 2015).

Spillover

  • Spillover effects occur when the situation or circumstances of one intended group influences other unintended groups (Schnittker et al. 2015)
  • For police:
    • Intended group: People in high-crime areas who have or are likely to commit crime.
      • relatively small group of individuals.
        • Total crime rate in Nashville in 2016: 4964.3/100,000 (UCR Index Crimes from MNPD)
    • Unintended group: Everyone else in the community.
  • Community health is one area where spillover may occur.

Policing Spillover on Health

  • Geller et al. (2014) show that young men who self-report intrusive police contact are at a greater risk of anxiety and trauma symptoms.
    • Study does not include measures of police activity or location.
  • Sewell, Jefferson, and Lee (2016) show that men living in highly policed areas—high rates of frisking and use of force—are more likely to show symptoms of psychological distress.
    • They use aggregated Stop and Frisk data in combination with a community health survey.
    • No self-report measure of police treatment
  • Sewell and Jefferson (2016) show that in highly policed areas, community members report a greater prevalence of poor/fair self-rated health, diabetes, high blood pressure, past year asthma episodes, and overweight/obesity.
  • Sewell (2017) showed that physical health links vary by the ethnoracial composition of neighborhoods.

Research Gaps

  • No research outside of NYC.
  • Only examines pedestrian stops from an unconstitutional policing program.
  • No studies have measured both policing practices and self-reports of unfair police treatment.

Present Study

  • Hypothesis 1: Individuals living in highly policed communities will have higher depressive symptoms than those in low police areas.
  • Hypothesis 2: Individuals reporting unfair police treatment will have higher depressive symptoms than those not reporting unfair police treatment.
  • Hypothesis 3: Unfair police treatment will moderate the effect of proactive policing such that reporting unfair treatment and living in highly policed areas will be associated with high depressive symptoms.

Methods

Nashville Context

Nashville Context

  • Nashville has been identified as a model police force that is using data-driven, targeted law enforcement (Weiss 2013)
  • Traffic stops and vehicle searches are concentrated in communities of color.

Data

  • Administrative records from MNPD
    • Traffic stops, includes searches
    • Arrests
    • Incidents
    • All Data sets include addresses which were geocoded in ArcGIS
  • Nashville Stress and Health Survey
    • Collected 2011-2013
    • Community multistage sample representative of county
      • Sampled 199 census block groups using 2000 boundaries
  • 2000 US Census demographics

Measures

Dependent Variable

  • Depressive Symptoms- 20-item CES-D (Logged)

Independent Variables

  • All crime and policing variables are calculated for one year prior to the survey date, use the 2000 block group population, and are logged.
  • Proactive policing:
    • Misdemeanor arrests per 1,000 residents
    • Vehicle searches by consent—without probable cause—per 1,000 residents
  • Violent Crime:
    • Robbery rate per 1,000 residents
    • Aggravated assault rate per 1,000 residents
  • Property Crime:
    • Burglary rate per 1,000 residents
    • Auto theft rate per 1,000 residents
  • Other Neighborhood Characteristics:
    • Median household income
    • Percent black residents

Measures (Cont.)

  • Individual variables -Unfair Treatment from Police
    • Item from Major Discrimination scale.
    • Have you “been unfairly treated by the police (e.g., stopped, searched, questioned, physically threatened or abused).”
      • Coded as two dummy variables:
        • Unfair treatment in past year
        • Unfair treatment more than a year ago
        • Reference: No unfair police treatment
    • Controls: Race, Gender, Age, Education, Employment, Marital Status, Parental status, Household income

Analysis

  • Weighted Least Squares regression.
    • Models estimated on full sample as well as split by race.
  • Interactions are estimated among unfair treatment variables and policing variables for blacks only.
    • Too few whites reported unfair treatment for stable estimates.

Discussion

  • Black Nashville Residents are more likely than whites to report unfair treatment from police, both recent and in the past.
    • They are also more likely to live in neighborhoods with high rates of proactive policing and crime
  • Reporting unfair police treatment is associated with psychological distress.
  • Living in areas with high rates of consent searches is associated with higher depressive symptoms.
    • Interactions show that consent searches are most harmful when the individual reports unfair police treatment.
  • Misdemeanor arrest rate is related to reduced depressive symptoms.
    • Interactions show fewer depressive symptoms in those who did not report unfair police treatment.

Conclusions

  • Some forms of proactive policing are associated with higher distress, especially when people have had poor experiences with police.
  • The direction of the arrest rate effect was unexpected. More research and analysis is needed.
  • Limitations
    • Not clear on mechanism
